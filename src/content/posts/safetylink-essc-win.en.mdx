On March 14th, my teammates Amber, Jerome, and I won the English Social Solutions Contest finals. First place. NTD 50,000 prize. It should have felt like a clean, satisfying win — and it did, for about ten minutes.

Then the adrenaline dropped and I started thinking about what the competition had actually forced me to do differently. Because building SafetyLink wasn't a comfortable process. It was clarifying in a way that was almost uncomfortable.

## The problem with being a CS student

Here's the honest version: when you spend years learning how to build complex things, you start measuring every solution against the complexity of what you can produce. An app feels more serious than a button. A model feels more serious than a rule. You conflate the sophistication of the tool with the quality of the outcome.

SafetyLink had no patience for that. We were building something for Taiwan's elderly population — over 600,000 seniors living alone, in a country already classified as a super-aged society. The use case was strokes. Falls. The moments where a person is on the floor and cannot think clearly, let alone navigate a UI or pair a Bluetooth device.

> The right answer was a single RF button that works autonomously. No app. No setup. No troubleshooting at the worst possible moment.

That wasn't a compromise. It was the whole point. Stripping away everything I would have instinctively added — that was the design decision. I don't think I would have arrived at it on my own, not without the constraint of actually having to defend it in front of judges, and more importantly, in front of the reality of who we were building for.

## What Amber and Jerome actually changed

Amber and Jerome come from an International Business background. Working with them on a technical project felt a little like switching languages mid-sentence at first. They asked questions I didn't think to ask — who manufactures this, at what cost, at what scale, and who do you actually have to convince to get it into homes?

We built out a full supply chain sourced from verified Taiwanese suppliers. We priced out the RF components down to commodity-level costs. That part of the project existed because of them — and it made the technical architecture matter in a different way. A one-button RF system is defensible not just because it's simple, but because it's manufacturable, cost-viable, and distributable.

I've worked in teams before, but this was the first time I genuinely felt the gap between having a technical solution and having a complete one. Those aren't the same thing, and I knew it abstractly, but now I know it in the way that actually sticks.

## The part I keep thinking about

During the final pitch, we talked about a hypothetical senior — someone who fell, couldn't get up, couldn't reach a phone. We'd been talking about "the user" throughout the project, but standing on stage and saying it out loud made it land differently.

This isn't hypothetical. This is happening. Every day, in apartments across Taiwan, someone is waiting for help that isn't coming fast enough. The generation that built this country's present is aging faster than the infrastructure around them is adapting.

I'm not naive about what a competition project is. SafetyLink isn't deployed anywhere. But the act of taking it seriously — sourcing the supply chain, pricing the hardware, designing the user experience around the worst moment of someone's day — changed how I think about what "done" means in engineering.

> Done isn't when it works. Done is when it works for the person who needs it most, in the conditions they're actually in.

That's what I'm taking out of this. Not the trophy. Not even the prize money — though I'd be lying if I said it didn't feel good. It's the recalibration. The reminder that the point of building things is not to demonstrate capability. It's to solve something real.

SafetyLink won the contest. But the sharper win was understanding why it was the right solution — and being able to articulate that clearly enough that a room full of judges agreed.

I'll be building on that standard going forward.

---

*SafetyLink was developed with teammates Amber and Jerome for the 2026 English Social Solutions Contest (ESSC) finals. The project targets Taiwan's growing elderly-alone population with an autonomous, one-button RF emergency alert system designed for reliability in high-stress, low-capability scenarios.*
